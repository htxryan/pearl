# Theme-Specific Structural Differentiation in Multi‑Theme Websites

*February 23, 2026*

## Abstract

This survey examines how multi‑theme websites can achieve theme‑specific “worlds” through structural differentiation rather than relying primarily on font and color substitutions, with a particular focus on brutalist and luxury aesthetics in digital interfaces. It documents how layout grids, hero compositions, navigation behaviors, card geometry, section dividers, visual textures, and interaction patterns can vary systematically while remaining compatible with a shared design system and codebase. The analysis spans theoretical foundations in grid systems, rhythm, negative space, and immersive world design, alongside practice‑oriented literature on design systems, multi‑brand theming, and style‑driven web design. [designsystems](https://www.designsystems.com/space-grids-and-layouts/)

The survey identifies three main families of approaches: token‑centric theming (primarily color and typography), component‑level structural theming (layout and interaction variants per theme), and world‑model theming inspired by immersive and virtual environments. These approaches exhibit trade‑offs in system complexity, expressive power, maintainability, and experiential distinctiveness, particularly when instantiating structurally raw brutalist themes and spacious, rhythmically controlled luxury themes within the same system. Evidence on multi‑theme systems remains fragmented across industry case studies, design guidelines, and emerging HCI research, which creates gaps regarding systematic evaluation and long‑term maintainability at scale. [designlab](https://designlab.com/blog/examples-brutalism-in-web-design)

## 1. Introduction

The problem addressed in this survey concerns how multi‑theme websites can make each theme feel like a distinct experiential “world” when sharing a single design system, instead of appearing as superficial variations in color and typography. This question matters for organizations operating multiple brands or audience modes (for example, brutalist marketing microsites adjacent to luxury product experiences), where thematic differentiation must coexist with shared infrastructure and coherent user journeys. The analysis documents how structural elements such as layout grids, hero compositions, navigation behaviors, card shapes, section dividers, surface textures, and interaction patterns contribute to this perceived world‑building, beyond purely stylistic tokens. [interaction-design](https://www.interaction-design.org/literature/topics/grid-systems)

The scope of this survey includes web‑based interfaces that adopt multiple visual or brand themes within a single system, with an emphasis on brutalist and luxury themes as contrasting poles along axes of visual noise, rhythm, and spatial density. In scope are design systems literature on spacing, grids, theming, and multi‑brand architectures, plus practice‑oriented discussions of brutalist and luxury‑like web styles, negative space, and rhythm. Explicitly excluded are topics such as generic dark/light mode theming, accessibility guidelines except where they intersect structural theming, and purely visual inspiration galleries that provide no mechanism‑level explanation. This exclusion is adopted to maintain focus on structural differentiation mechanisms and their integration into systems. [todaymade](https://www.todaymade.com/blog/brutalist-web-design)

Several key definitions clarify the discussion. In plain terms, a “theme” is a coherent visual and behavioral mood that shapes how an interface looks and feels; conceptually, this corresponds to a parameterized configuration of tokens, components, and behaviors; formally, it is referred to as a **theme specification** in design system literature. “Structural differentiation” means that different themes change the spatial organization, interaction dynamics, and component geometry, rather than only swapping colors or fonts; conceptually, this involves theme‑specific grid systems, densities, and navigation structures, which this survey calls **structural theming**. Finally, “world‑like theming” refers to the creation of distinct experiential spaces that resemble separate worlds in games or themed environments, where layout, rhythm, and interactions align with a narrative or identity; conceptually, this extends to hub‑and‑world architectures documented in immersive design research and is referred to here as **world‑model theming**. [blog.prototypr](https://blog.prototypr.io/common-pitfalls-in-design-systems-overcoming-grid-challenges-semantic-naming-accessibility-ba37f93f9940)

## 2. Foundations

Foundational concepts in layout design and design systems provide the theoretical basis for structural differentiation across themes. Grid systems organize content through intersecting vertical and horizontal lines that define columns, gutters, and margins, enabling designers to create balanced, coherent layouts and consistent information hierarchies. These grids vary in complexity from single‑column arrangements to complex multi‑column systems, and include canonical variants such as golden‑section‑inspired grids, modular grids, and responsive grids that adjust element placement for different screen sizes. This structural scaffolding establishes a baseline that themes can either respect or intentionally violate, which becomes especially relevant when contrasting brutalist rejection of conventional grids with luxury designs that emphasize refined alignment and proportional spacing. [toptal](https://www.toptal.com/designers/ux/minimalist-brutalist-web-design)

Rhythm and negative space are further critical foundations for theme‑specific structure. Visual rhythm involves repeated patterns with variation that guide the eye through a layout, shaping pacing and perceived dynamism or calmness. Some analyses describe rhythm as arising from controlled repetition, proximity, and texture, allowing designers to influence where users look first, where they pause, and how long they dwell on particular elements. Negative space, defined as the empty areas around and between elements, is documented as a primary mechanism for establishing hierarchy, clarity, and brand tone, particularly in interfaces seeking a premium or luxurious feel. Luxury‑oriented UI examples appear to use generous margins and “floaty” layouts, whereas denser dashboards use tight spacing and compressed rhythm to signal information richness; this contrast maps closely onto structural theming goals when differentiating worlds. [pageflows](https://pageflows.com/resources/principles-of-design-rhythm/)

Design systems and theming architectures underpin multi‑theme implementations. Contemporary design systems are described as living sets of standards and reusable components that manage design at scale across brands and channels, often with explicit separation between foundational tokens and higher‑level components. Industry literature on multi‑brand systems characterizes the challenge as balancing shared core foundations (for example, spacing scales, component anatomy) with brand or theme specific expression layers, often implemented via parallel token libraries in tools such as Figma. Token‑based theming approaches represent colors, typography, and other style attributes as semantic tokens, which can be remapped for each theme while preserving structural relationships; some authors emphasize hierarchical naming schemes and separate static versus theme‑aware tokens, especially for colors. These foundations explain why color‑only theming is relatively straightforward, but they also reveal the need for additional mechanisms when themes must alter layouts, navigation structures, or interaction densities. [nngroup](https://www.nngroup.com/articles/design-systems-vs-style-guides/)

Finally, research on immersive and themed environments offers a conceptual bridge to world‑like theming. Work on themed and immersive spaces, including theme parks and educational virtual worlds, describes how environments are structured into hubs and worlds, each with distinctive spatial layouts, textures, and interaction affordances that support embodiment, curiosity, and narrative coherence. In these models, “hub worlds” act as directories that connect multiple themed spaces, each functioning as a separate world with different layouts and experiential cues. This structure parallels the idea of a multi‑theme website whose global shell acts as a hub while each theme instantiates its own structural logic, suggesting a basis for the world‑model theming approach described later in §3 and §4.3. [studyingthemeparks](https://studyingthemeparks.com/wp-content/uploads/2018/10/a_reader_in_themed_and_immersive_spaces.pdf)

Each approach is analyzed in §4 with respect to theory, evidence, implementations, and trade‑offs.

## 4. Analysis

### 4.1 Token‑Centric Theming with Structural Presets

#### Theory & mechanism

Token‑centric theming with structural presets conceptualizes themes as alternative configurations of design tokens that parameterize a shared component library, occasionally combined with limited per‑theme layout presets such as density or spacing modes. Design tokens in this context capture semantic abstractions for color, typography, and spacing (for example, text‑primary, surface‑background, spacing‑xl), enabling designers to remap visual appearance without modifying component structure or markup. Some system descriptions distinguish primitive tokens (raw values) from semantic tokens (contextual roles), arguing that themes should primarily manipulate semantic tokens within a predefined schema, which allows consistent behavior across brands and themes. Structural presets layer on top of this by exposing a small set of parameters, such as compact versus comfortable density or alternative grid breakpoints, which can be toggled per theme while maintaining a single underlying layout model. [youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=8rDVg9Zz1fY)

From a mechanism perspective, this approach constrains structural differentiation to parametric shifts in spacing, line heights, and component size, occasionally including alternative container widths or hero height presets. Analyses of spacing systems document how consistent spatial units and vertical rhythm can be defined once and reused across layouts, which token‑centric theming then remaps to produce different experiential tones. For example, a “luxury” theme might map large spacing tokens to generous paddings and wide margins, thereby producing a more spacious rhythm, while a “brutalist” theme might compress vertical spacing and increase typographic scale within the same grid. The structural grammar remains identical, however, so perceived world differences arise primarily from scale, proportion, and visual weight rather than from distinct compositional logics. This explains why such systems often struggle to make themes feel like separate worlds when aesthetics differ strongly. [uxdesign](https://uxdesign.cc/flexible-styles-for-multi-brand-design-systems-638f9c25c227)

#### Literature evidence

Industry‑oriented discussions of design systems frequently present token‑centric theming as the default approach for supporting multiple brands or themes. A multi‑brand case study describes constructing theme‑specific foundation libraries in Figma, feeding color and asset variations into shared components while preserving their structural definitions, and highlights modular semantic naming as a key tool for scalable theming. Another account explains that modern design systems often separate static colors from theme‑dependent ones, using semantic tokens whose expressions may depend on a base “lightness” or contrast coefficient, which enables light, dark, and brand themes without altering component structure. A theming design article similarly argues that themes should conform to a schema specifying property names and data types for tokens, noting that hierarchy in theme properties clarifies usage and prevents misuse, such as applying background colors to typography roles. [dev](https://dev.to/danieldelcore/how-to-approach-theming-a-design-system-5829)

Empirical studies on design systems themselves remain limited, but there is broader HCI literature on layout and UI recommendations that supports the importance of token‑level consistency. A usability‑focused synthesis identifies categories of UI recommendations organized by generic and specific principles, including consistency, readability, and visual hierarchy, which token‑centric systems aim to encode in reusable form. Additionally, introductory design system case studies for startups emphasize building consistent spacing, typography, and color foundations before component structures, suggesting that token‑centric approaches are widely adopted in practice even where empirical evaluation is sparse. Evidence specific to structural presets in theming (for example, density modes or alternate grid breakpoints) remains largely anecdotal, appearing in blog posts and talks rather than controlled studies, which indicates a gap in rigorous evaluation. [humanfactors.jmir](https://humanfactors.jmir.org/2022/3/e37894/PDF)

#### Implementations & benchmarks

In practice, token‑centric theming is exemplified by popular component libraries and design systems that emphasize configurable themes through tokens. Documentation for major systems describes color and typography tokens that can be overridden per theme while components remain structurally identical, with theming APIs exposing palette and typography slots rather than layout definitions. Case study material on building a design system for a startup shows a process where spacing scales and grids are defined centrally, then applied consistently across screens, with theme variations primarily affecting colors and fonts rather than layout structures. Multi‑channel design system presentations document parallel foundation libraries (for example, per brand or theme) feeding into shared platform‑specific component libraries, with library swapping enabling quick changes to color, typography, and assets while preserving layout and interaction patterns. [diva-portal](https://www.diva-portal.org/smash/get/diva2:1592026/FULLTEXT01.pdf)

Quantitative benchmarks for token‑centric theming are largely absent; most available metrics concern usability or aesthetic judgments of specific instances rather than comparative evaluations of theming strategies. A study on automated UI color theme generation reports user evaluations of color themes derived from images, finding that themes generated by their model were perceived as consistent with source imagery and aesthetically pleasing, but it does not address structural differences or multi‑theme experiences within a single site. Usability evaluations of UI redesigns, such as those using design thinking for e‑commerce interfaces, tend to report System Usability Scale scores for a single design variant, leaving open questions about how token‑centric theme variants compare in terms of user perception of distinct worlds. Consequently, evidence regarding how effectively token‑centric theming alone creates world‑like differentiation remains weak and largely inferential. [journal.multitechpublisher](https://journal.multitechpublisher.com/index.php/ijatss/article/download/1532/1704)

#### Strengths & limitations

Token‑centric theming with structural presets demonstrates several strengths, particularly in maintainability and implementation simplicity. Because component structures and layout grammars remain shared, designers and developers can manage a single component set and rely on tokens to express brand or theme variation, which decreases code duplication and reduces the risk of divergence. This approach scales relatively well when the number of themes increases, provided themes accept common structural constraints, and it integrates cleanly with popular tooling for design tokens and multi‑brand design systems. When themes differ modestly, such as between light and dark modes, or across closely related brands, token‑centric systems appear sufficient to produce coherent differentiation without compromising consistency or accessibility. [blog.prototypr](https://blog.prototypr.io/common-pitfalls-in-design-systems-overcoming-grid-challenges-semantic-naming-accessibility-ba37f93f9940)

The limitations become more pronounced when themes are expected to embody radically different structural personalities, such as brutalist versus luxury worlds. Because layout grids, component anatomy, and navigation logic remain largely fixed, token‑centric theming may only suggest differences through color, typography, and density, which can cause themes to feel like skins rather than distinct worlds. Attempting to push structural differentiation through tokens alone (for example, heavily modifying spacing scales per theme) can introduce constraints conflicts and unforeseen layout breakages, especially when content and component combinations vary widely. The absence of robust empirical evaluation on user perception of world‑likeness under token‑centric theming further limits confidence about its ability to support highly divergent thematic personalities at scale. This suggests that token‑centric theming is more suited to scenarios where structural variation is secondary to brand coloration and typography rather than where themes must feel architecturally distinct. [blog.tubikstudio](https://blog.tubikstudio.com/negative-space-in-design-tips-and-best-practices/)

### 4.2 Component‑Level Structural Theming

#### Theory & mechanism

Component‑level structural theming conceptualizes each theme as a family of structural variants built on shared conceptual abstractions, such as cards, navigation modules, and hero patterns. Instead of only swapping tokens, this approach defines theme‑specific component implementations that may alter layout, proportions, internal grids, and interactive behaviors while maintaining compatibility at the level of data contracts and content semantics. For example, a “card” abstraction might have a brutalist variant using hard edges, large typography, and minimal padding, alongside a luxury variant featuring rounded corners, generous white space, and layered elevations, each encoded as separate structural variants within the same system. Similarly, navigation components may differ between themes, with brutalist designs intentionally disrupting conventional information hierarchies and alignment, and luxury designs emphasizing calm, predictable navigation experiences. [todaymade](https://www.todaymade.com/blog/brutalist-web-design)

This approach relies on the notion that design systems can encapsulate not only shared patterns but also theme‑specific pattern families, enabling more pronounced structural differentiation while preserving overall cohesion. Discussions of design systems versus style guides emphasize that mature systems include patterns and interactions, not just visual style, which aligns with the idea of structural variants as first‑class citizens within a system. Layout research on constraint‑based systems and exploration tools, such as systems that support rapid generation of layout alternatives via high‑level design constraints, suggests that structurally distinct variants can be formalized as configurations within a design space defined by constraints on alignment, emphasis, and order. Component‑level structural theming uses this conceptual framework to define per‑theme defaults for properties like grid alignment, whitespace distribution, and interaction density, while maintaining shared semantics and usability baselines. [arxiv](http://arxiv.org/pdf/2001.05424.pdf)

#### Literature evidence

Evidence for component‑level structural theming appears in case studies and conceptual discussions of multi‑brand design systems. A multi‑brand design system narrative describes building a single system for multiple brands and platforms by separating foundational artifacts from platform‑specific component libraries and leveraging tools for library swapping; this structure enables the creation of brand‑specific component variants that share underlying semantics while differing structurally. Another case study on integrating a design system with a CMS highlights that component abstractions can be reused across different site experiences while allowing theme‑specific templates and component configurations, which supports more substantive layout differences between themes. These accounts consistently emphasize that as design systems grow to support multiple brands or themes, complexity shifts from tokens to component patterns and compositions, implying that component‑level structural theming is both necessary and challenging. [blog.sparkfabrik](https://blog.sparkfabrik.com/en/design-system-and-drupal-cms)

Brutalist and luxury‑like web design literature provides qualitative evidence regarding structural traits that component variants must capture. Analyses of brutalist web design describe raw and unpolished layouts, often featuring minimal color palettes, large typography, deliberate asymmetry, and non‑standard navigation that rejects conventional UI hierarchy. These sources repeatedly note that brutalist websites may present content in stark, often monochrome environments, with exposed interface elements resembling early web designs, large headlines, and intentionally awkward spacing, which together produce a distinct structural feel. In contrast, discussions of negative space and premium UI emphasize generous whitespace, careful rhythm, and refined balance, where spacious layouts and controlled density signal a high‑end, calm experience. These contrasting structural profiles suggest that component‑level variants are essential for capturing brutalist rawness and luxury spaciousness within a shared system. [designmantic](https://www.designmantic.com/blog/brutalism-in-web-design/)

#### Implementations & benchmarks

Specific implementations of component‑level structural theming can be observed in large‑scale design systems and front‑end frameworks, although detailed public documentation is limited. Articles on design systems versus style guides emphasize that systems like those of major tech companies include platform‑specific component sets and themes that differ structurally across use cases, even when sharing fundamental tokens. Multi‑theme case studies for enterprise platforms describe maintaining parallel component libraries or extending base components with brand‑specific variants that alter layout and interaction details; video material on multi‑theme systems illustrates this via parallel foundation libraries feeding into distinct component libraries for each brand or theme. These implementations demonstrate that component‑level structural theming is feasible and used in practice, especially in multi‑brand ecosystems, but they rarely provide systematic benchmarks. [nngroup](https://www.nngroup.com/articles/design-systems-vs-style-guides/)

Empirical benchmarks for component‑level structural theming are sparse, though related work in interface layout exploration and customization offers indirect evidence. Research on systems that enable users to customize overview‑detail interfaces reports that participants produce diverse customizations and unique usage patterns, highlighting demand for flexible composition and layout transformations and suggesting that controlled structural variation can positively impact user fit. Another system for rapid exploration of interface layout alternatives through high‑level constraints shows that presenting designers with diverse layout options accelerates exploration and may lead to more varied design outcomes. While these studies do not directly measure theme‑specific structural variants across brands, they indicate that structurally distinct layouts grounded in shared abstractions can be generated and used effectively, supporting the feasibility of component‑level structural theming from a tooling perspective. [arxiv](https://arxiv.org/html/2503.07782v1)

#### Strengths & limitations

Component‑level structural theming appears to offer a stronger capacity for experiential differentiation than token‑centric approaches, particularly when themes represent divergent personalities such as brutalist and luxury aesthetics. Because components can embody theme‑specific grid usage, alignment norms, card geometry, and interaction behaviors (for example, hover animations, transitions), each theme can present a distinct structural signature while preserving shared semantics and content models. This enables, for instance, a brutalist theme whose navigation deliberately disrupts hierarchy and uses oversized, raw typography, alongside a luxury theme whose navigation is understated, aligned, and rhythmically spaced, both built on a common navigation abstraction. Additionally, encapsulating theme‑specific structure at the component level confines complexity, reducing the need for page‑level divergence and allowing targeted adjustments where they most affect perceived personality. [designlab](https://designlab.com/blog/examples-brutalism-in-web-design)

However, component‑level structural theming introduces significant complexity in design system governance and implementation. As the number of themes grows, maintaining parallel component variants can become costly, especially when changes to shared semantics require synchronized updates across all structural variants. There is also a risk that divergent component structures will erode consistency and confuse users moving between themes if patterns diverge too far from shared mental models or usability guidelines. Evidence on long‑term maintainability and user perception of cross‑theme consistency is limited, and most documented cases rely on expert judgment rather than controlled user studies. Consequently, while component‑level structural theming provides a mechanism for stronger world‑like differentiation than token‑centric approaches, it also raises unresolved questions about complexity, scalability, and evaluation. [matec-conferences](https://www.matec-conferences.org/articles/matecconf/pdf/2018/23/matecconf_icesti2018_01006.pdf)

### 4.3 World‑Model Theming

#### Theory & mechanism

World‑model theming treats each theme as an internally coherent “world” with its own structural grammar for pages, sections, and interactions, connected via a shared hub or shell that manages navigation and cross‑theme coherence. This approach draws inspiration from themed and immersive environments, particularly theme parks and educational virtual worlds, where multiple distinct worlds are accessed through a hub and each world exhibits unique spatial layouts, textures, and interaction affordances aligned with a narrative. Theoretical models of immersive spaces describe characteristics such as embodiment, role‑playing, curiosity, emotional response, and shared experience as drivers of engagement, and present core world models (for example, hub worlds, linear worlds, branching worlds) as building blocks for designing immersive experiences. World‑model theming adapts this logic by defining per‑theme page archetypes, section rhythms, and navigation flows that function as world models for web experiences, rather than limiting differentiation to components. [ulaw.repository.guildhe.ac](https://ulaw.repository.guildhe.ac.uk/id/eprint/29/1/Interactive%20Storytelling%20through%20Immersive%20Design%20-%20VanFossen%20-%20GibsonHylands.pdf)

Mechanistically, world‑model theming often combines component‑level structural variants with theme‑specific page templates and navigation structures. The hub (for example, a global shell or main landing page) acts as a directory that links to different themed worlds, analogous to theme park hubs that route visitors to different lands. Within each world, layouts may adhere to distinct grid logics, rhythm patterns, and interaction densities that reflect the theme’s personality; for instance, brutalist worlds may feature intentionally fragmented grids, exposed structural lines, large hero blocks with raw typography, and unexpected animations or cursor treatments, whereas luxury worlds may prioritize symmetrical or carefully balanced asymmetrical compositions, ample negative space, and smooth, restrained interaction patterns. This approach shifts structural differentiation from isolated components to the overall composition and narrative flow, aligning layout and navigation behaviors with thematic identity. [youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=n-4FmlonPjc)

#### Literature evidence

Direct academic literature on world‑model theming for websites is limited, but adjacent research on themed and immersive spaces provides conceptual grounding. A reader on themed and immersive spaces discusses how architectural movements like brutalism use rich, exposed textures and structural expressionism to create distinctive spatial identities, and how theme‑based environments leverage narrative, spatial sequencing, and sensory cues to produce immersion. A study on interactive storytelling through immersive design introduces the DEW concept model, describing how different world formats (including hub worlds) support educational experiences by structuring exploration and wonder responses, and documents how hub worlds act as directories connecting multiple thematic spaces that students can explore. Furthermore, commentary on recent theme park expansions reports that hub and spoke layouts with strongly themed worlds improve navigability while preserving distinct identities for each world, illustrating how structural organization supports both wayfinding and thematic immersion. [e3s-conferences](https://www.e3s-conferences.org/articles/e3sconf/pdf/2021/07/e3sconf_peee2021_04001.pdf)

Brutalist web design articles and examples highlight how structural choices create world‑like experiences. These sources emphasize that brutalist web design rejects ornamentation, embraces raw, stripped‑down layouts, and often uses monochrome palettes, oversized typography, deliberate asymmetry, and exposed interface elements, resulting in interfaces that feel unpolished and highly intentional. Some analyses describe brutalist websites as “angry teenagers” of design, intentionally ignoring conventional UI fundamentals, including standard grids and navigation patterns, in order to challenge expectations and foreground content or message. In contrast, discussions of negative space and rhythm in UI design document how luxury‑like interfaces create calm, premium experiences through generous whitespace, controlled pacing, and balanced visual cadence, with examples contrasting “floaty” wellness apps against dense data dashboards. Collectively, these sources suggest that world‑model theming can draw from immersive world theories and style‑specific structural traits to define theme‑specific worlds. [carlbarenbrug](https://carlbarenbrug.com/brutalist-web-design)

#### Implementations & benchmarks

Explicit implementations of world‑model theming remain largely in industry practice and case‑specific experiences, rather than in generic frameworks. Themed entertainment design and large content platforms sometimes adopt hub‑and‑world architectures, where a central landing area routes users into distinct themed sections, each with its own layout patterns and interaction styles, but systematic documentation is scarce. In digital product contexts, some multi‑channel design system presentations describe creating different experiences per platform or brand that share foundations but diverge more significantly at the template and page structure level, suggesting an implicit world‑model approach, although these descriptions focus on workflow and governance rather than on user perception. Additionally, research on malleable overview‑detail interfaces and layout exploration indicates that systems can support multiple layout paradigms within a single application, which aligns conceptually with offering different world models under a shared shell. [ucf](https://www.ucf.edu/news/epic-universe-launch-provides-immersive-learning-opportunities-at-ucf/)

Empirical benchmarks for world‑model theming are essentially absent; related work primarily evaluates immersion and engagement in VR or themed educational environments. Studies applying the DEW model to educational virtual worlds report that students using world formats aligned with the model exhibit strong connections to subject matter and engagement with exploration and discovery, but these results concern VR environments rather than multi‑theme websites. Theme park oriented analyses and reports describe qualitative benefits of distinct themed lands connected through hubs, such as improved immersion and clearer mental models of park layout, yet do not quantify these outcomes for digital analogues. Since there is little direct research on web‑based world‑model theming, the evidence base remains thin and requires extrapolation from adjacent domains, which introduces uncertainty regarding transferability to standard web interfaces. [facebook](https://www.facebook.com/groups/universalepicuniversethemepark/posts/1017117347152902/)

#### Strengths & limitations

World‑model theming offers the highest potential for themes to feel like distinct worlds because it allows divergence at the level of page structure, navigation flows, and experiential pacing. By defining theme‑specific world grammars that govern hero compositions, section sequencing, visual textures, and interaction patterns, this approach can create brutalist worlds that feel structurally raw and confrontational and luxury worlds that feel spacious and measured, all within a single product ecosystem. Tying these worlds together through a hub or shell preserves high‑level coherence and aids navigation, leveraging patterns documented in immersive space design and theme park layouts where hubs act as orienting anchors. The approach also aligns with the way users may already conceptualize brand universes across touchpoints, potentially making world‑like differentiation intuitive when executed consistently. [pageflows](https://pageflows.com/resources/principles-of-design-rhythm/)

The limitations of world‑model theming are substantial in terms of complexity, cost, and risk. Implementing distinct world grammars per theme involves designing and maintaining multiple sets of templates, navigation structures, and interaction patterns, which can approach the complexity of separate products, particularly as content grows. This complexity raises challenges for accessibility, consistency, and maintainability, as ensuring that each world meets usability and performance standards requires substantial ongoing investment. Furthermore, because empirical evidence for web‑based world‑model theming is minimal, there is limited guidance on how users perceive cross‑world coherence, how cognitive load is affected by moving between structurally distinct worlds, and how best to manage transitions. Consequently, while world‑model theming holds promise for maximal experiential differentiation, the current evidence base is thin, and its scalability beyond carefully curated experiences remains uncertain. [arxiv](https://arxiv.org/html/2503.07782v1)

## 6. Open Problems & Gaps

First, there is a clear lack of empirical research on user perception of theme‑specific structural differentiation in multi‑theme websites. Existing studies evaluate UI guidelines, layout exploration tools, or immersive VR worlds, but none systematically compare token‑centric, component‑level, and world‑model theming in terms of perceived world‑likeness, cognitive load, or brand differentiation. Resolving this gap would require controlled experiments that manipulate theming approach and measure outcomes such as user recognition of distinct worlds, task performance, and long‑term memory of theme characteristics. [humanfactors.jmir](https://humanfactors.jmir.org/2022/3/e37894/PDF)

Second, the long‑term maintainability and governance of world‑model theming remain under‑documented. Design systems literature highlights challenges arising from complex multi‑brand systems, yet there is little evidence on how organizations manage multiple world grammars, especially when content changes, legal requirements evolve, or accessibility standards tighten. Addressing this gap would demand longitudinal case studies that document how teams adapt, refactor, or consolidate world models over time, including metrics on maintenance cost, defect rates, and cross‑world inconsistencies. [diva-portal](https://www.diva-portal.org/smash/get/diva2:1592026/FULLTEXT01.pdf)

Third, the interaction between structural theming and accessibility and usability guidelines is insufficiently explored. UI recommendation frameworks and usability studies identify numerous principles and patterns, but it is unclear how far structural differentiation can diverge (particularly in brutalist worlds) before violating usability or accessibility expectations, especially for navigation and information hierarchy. Research that systematically varies structural traits within themes and tests them against accessibility and usability measures would clarify boundaries and trade‑offs between expressive world‑building and inclusive design. [matec-conferences](https://www.matec-conferences.org/articles/matecconf/pdf/2018/23/matecconf_icesti2018_01006.pdf)

Fourth, there is limited guidance on formalizing theme‑specific world grammars in design systems tooling. While some literature describes theme specifications for tokens and semantic naming, there is no widely accepted schema for encoding per‑theme layout patterns, rhythm rules, or world models within design systems, nor for connecting them to component libraries. Developing formal representations and associated tooling, potentially extending constraint‑based layout systems and malleable interfaces, could enable more systematic and reusable world‑model theming. [arxiv](https://arxiv.org/html/2503.00591v1)

Fifth, cross‑channel consistency of world‑like themes across web, mobile, and immersive platforms is under‑researched. Multi‑channel design system accounts describe foundations feeding into platform‑specific libraries, but they do not analyze how structural differentiation per theme translates between, for example, responsive web layouts and mobile apps or VR experiences. Investigating how world‑model theming and component‑level structural variants can be coherently expressed across channels would require comparative studies combining HCI methods with design system engineering. [uxdesign](https://uxdesign.cc/flexible-styles-for-multi-brand-design-systems-638f9c25c227)

## 7. Conclusion

This survey documents a spectrum of approaches for achieving theme‑specific structural differentiation in multi‑theme websites, ranging from token‑centric theming with structural presets, through component‑level structural theming, to world‑model theming informed by immersive environment design. Each approach embodies a different balance between structural expressiveness and implementation complexity, with token‑centric systems emphasizing maintainability, component‑level approaches emphasizing flexible pattern families, and world‑model theming emphasizing fully immersive, world‑like experiences. Across these approaches, themes such as brutalist rawness and luxury spaciousness manifest not only in color and typography but in grid usage, rhythm, navigation behavior, and interaction density, which together shape the experiential sense of separate worlds. [designsystems](https://www.designsystems.com/space-grids-and-layouts/)

The analysis reveals that current practice heavily favors token‑centric and component‑level structural theming, reflecting strong tooling support and manageable complexity, while world‑model theming remains conceptually compelling but empirically under‑explored and operationally demanding. Evidence from immersive design, theme parks, and layout customization research suggests that world‑like theming can enhance engagement and differentiation, but rigorous studies on web‑based multi‑theme systems are largely absent, particularly regarding user perception, accessibility, and long‑term maintenance. Future work that integrates design system engineering, HCI experimentation, and immersive world theories appears necessary to clarify how multi‑theme websites can systematically make each theme feel like its own world rather than a superficial font or color substitution.
