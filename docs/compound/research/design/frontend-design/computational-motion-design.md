# Computational Motion Design for Interactive Media

*22 February 2026*

## Abstract

This survey examines **computational** motion design at the intersection of physics simulation, procedural animation, and real‑time graphics for interactive media, with a focus on particle systems, fluid‑like effects, shader‑driven motion, procedural generation, physics‑based easing, and perceptually plausible “alive” motion. It targets practitioners who want to ground aesthetic motion decisions in physically and perceptually motivated models while retaining real‑time performance on contemporary GPUs. [itn-web.it.liu](https://itn-web.it.liu.se/~stegu76/TNM084-2019/Insights_ch7.pdf)

The document first lays theoretical groundwork in numerical physics, particle and continuum methods, and basic motion perception, then proposes a taxonomy spanning continuum fluid approaches, particle methods, shader‑based field animation, procedural geometry systems, and physically inspired controllers for timing and easing. Each class is analyzed in terms of theory, empirical evidence, representative implementations, and characteristic strengths and limitations. The survey then synthesizes trade‑offs in terms of visual richness, control, performance, stability, and perceived animacy, and identifies gaps such as limited integration of psychophysical findings into tooling, weak authoring abstractions for complex coupled phenomena, and sparse empirical evaluation linking parameter spaces to user-perceived qualities like “alive,” “mechanical,” or “magical.” [pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC10273680/)

## 1. Introduction

- Problem statement
  Interactive media increasingly expects rich, responsive motion—trails, explosions, fluid‑like surfaces, aurora‑style fields, and “alive” UI transitions—yet most production workflows still rely on ad‑hoc easing curves, hand‑tuned shaders, and black‑box physics components. The challenge is to structure the design space: which computational approaches are available, how they relate theoretically, how they map onto GPU/CPU architectures, and how they interact with human motion perception.

- Scope: covered
  - Real‑time or near‑real‑time motion for interactive graphics (games, installations, web/WebGL).
  - Particle systems and fluid‑like simulations for visual effects (e.g., SPH‑like, grid‑based, and hybrid particle–grid methods, but from a motion‑design rather than engineering perspective). [arxiv](http://arxiv.org/pdf/2405.09672.pdf)
  - Shader programming for motion (vertex/fragment/compute shaders, procedural displacement, time‑varying fields). [blog.pixelfreestudio](https://blog.pixelfreestudio.com/how-to-use-glsl-shaders-in-webgl-for-advanced-3d-effects/)
  - Procedural generation systems (noise‑based fields, fractals, L‑systems) as drivers of motion rather than only static content. [itn-web.it.liu](https://itn-web.it.liu.se/~stegu76/TNM084-2019/Insights_ch7.pdf)
  - Physics‑based easing (springs, damped oscillators, constraint‑based controllers) beyond simple cubic‑Bezier curves. [dl.acm](https://dl.acm.org/doi/fullHtml/10.1145/3582437.3582485)
  - Perception and psychology of motion: animacy, anticipation, follow‑through, plausibility of exaggerated motion. [sciencedirect](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0010027724001665)

- Scope: excluded
  - Offline feature‑film simulation pipelines (large‑scale CFD for VFX) except as conceptual background.
  - Robotics control and physically based character locomotion beyond simple ragdoll‑like or articulated examples.
  - Photoreal fluid engineering CFD; we only import concepts needed to reason about stylized and approximate effects. [en.wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Computational_fluid_dynamics)

- Key definitions
  - Computational motion design: the use of algorithmic, typically real‑time, methods to generate, control, and render motion as part of an interactive visual experience.
  - Procedural animation: animation generated by rules or algorithms (noise, L‑systems, solvers) rather than baked keyframes.
  - Physically based / physics‑inspired: models that approximate physical laws (e.g., Navier–Stokes, Hooke’s law) or adopt their qualitative structure (inertia, damping) even if numerically simplified. [icemm](https://www.icemm.com/en/particle-methods-in-cfd-simulation/)
  - Animacy: the perceptual impression that motion is produced by an agent or living system, influenced by specific kinematic signatures. [pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC10273680/)

## 2. Foundations

### 2.1 Numerical physics for visual motion

Continuum fluid simulation traditionally solves discretized forms of the Navier–Stokes equations for incompressible or compressible flow on grids using finite volume, finite difference, or finite element methods; these techniques inform many stylized fluid‑like effects. Particle methods like Smoothed Particle Hydrodynamics (SPH) instead approximate fluid quantities with moving particles carrying mass, position, and sometimes additional attributes, which suits highly dynamic, free‑surface phenomena in graphics. Hybrid Eulerian–Lagrangian schemes such as Particle Flow Maps combine grid‑based solvers with Lagrangian particles to handle long‑range advection while retaining stability and detail, which is attractive for real‑time smoke or aurora‑like flows. [dewberry](https://www.dewberry.com/insights-news/post/blog/2021/01/21/computational-fluid-dynamics-five-applications-of-fluid-motion-in-three-dimensions)

Classical “graphics” particle systems, as used in games, decouple from strict physics to favor control and performance, using simplified integration (e.g., semi‑implicit Euler), ad‑hoc forces, and life‑cycle rules for emissions, collisions, and fade‑outs. Multi‑particle collision dynamics (MPC) and related mesoscale methods show how more complex behaviors (e.g., non‑ideal fluids, phase separation) can emerge from simple, local collision rules, illustrating how relatively low‑level computational rules can produce rich motion fields. For motion design, these foundational methods inform a spectrum between “physically grounded” and “design‑driven,” with many practical systems sitting in the middle. [arxiv](http://arxiv.org/pdf/0808.2157.pdf)

### 2.2 Shader programming and GPU execution

Modern motion design runs primarily on the GPU, with vertex and fragment shaders (and increasingly compute shaders) used to implement both visual appearance and motion. GLSL and similar languages expose time, positions, normals, and arbitrary uniforms, letting designers treat motion as a function of space and time—e.g., wave‑like displacement of vertices driven by sine functions of position and a global time uniform. Procedural surface and displacement shaders, especially when animated via time‑dependent texture coordinates or 3D noise fields, can produce motion that resembles fluid, cloth, or organic growth without explicit simulation. [blog.pixelfreestudio](https://blog.pixelfreestudio.com/how-to-use-glsl-shaders-in-webgl-for-advanced-3d-effects/)

WebGL brings this capability to the browser, where fragment shaders can operate on full‑screen quads, using ray‑marching, signed distance fields, and noise to generate volumetric‑like effects and complex motion fields in real time. An emerging pattern is to combine GPU‑resident fields (e.g., velocity textures) with lightweight CPU‑side controllers (e.g., GSAP timelines driving uniforms) for interactive control over continuous, shader‑defined motion. [arxiv](https://arxiv.org/html/2512.08951)

### 2.3 Perception of motion, animacy, and believability

Motion perception research shows that humans infer animacy from specific kinematic patterns even in very simple stimuli, such as moving dots or geometric shapes. Attributes like self‑propelled motion, sudden changes in speed or direction not explainable by external forces, and coordinated interactions between objects (e.g., chasing, avoiding) strongly bias perception toward “alive.” Work on perceptual plausibility of exaggerated realistic motion, such as bouncing balls with squash‑and‑stretch timing, demonstrates that physically impossible deformations can remain plausible or even more “believable” when timed relative to impact and velocity in specific ways. [sciencedirect](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0010027724001665)

Disney’s principles of anticipation, action, and follow‑through align with more recent formal models that decompose action into stages with distinct timing signatures. In Michotte’s launching effect, perception of causal contact depends on tight temporal contiguity (delays above roughly 140 ms break the impression of one object launching another) and speed ratios between colliding objects. These findings suggest that motion design systems should expose parameters not only in physical units but also in perceptually meaningful ranges (e.g., delay thresholds, exaggeration windows, speed ratios) to support “alive versus mechanical” tuning. [dl.acm](https://dl.acm.org/doi/fullHtml/10.1145/3582437.3582485)

## 3. Taxonomy of Approaches

We propose the following taxonomy for computational motion design in interactive media:

1. Continuum fluid and field simulation
2. Particle‑based motion systems
3. Shader‑driven procedural fields
4. Procedural geometry and structure‑growth systems
5. Physics‑based easing and spring dynamics
6. Perception‑driven motion controllers

## 4. Analysis

### 4.1 Continuum fluid and field simulation

- **Theory & mechanism**
  Continuum fluid methods approximate Navier–Stokes equations over a discretized domain, solving for velocity, pressure, and sometimes additional scalar fields (e.g., temperature, dye). Standard pipelines include advection, diffusion, external forces, and pressure projection to enforce incompressibility, often with semi‑Lagrangian advection for stability at larger time steps. Hybrid Eulerian–Lagrangian fluid simulation on particle flow maps uses Lagrangian particles to encode flow maps while still solving core equations on a grid, enabling accurate long‑range advection and bidirectional coupling in a form well‑suited to interactive scenarios. [arxiv](http://arxiv.org/pdf/2405.09672.pdf)

- **Literature evidence**
  In graphics, grid‑based incompressible flow solvers have long been used for smoke, fire, and cloud‑like effects; more recent work emphasizes real‑time reconstruction of fluid flow from sparse inputs, using SPH and gradient‑based optimization to match observed kinematics under unknown disturbances. Studies of particle–fluid coupling (CFD–DEM, DEM–LBM, etc.) provide insight into how particle‑laden flows behave, useful for grounded stylized sprays or particle‑rich plumes. [mdpi](https://www.mdpi.com/2305-7084/4/4/55/pdf)

- **Implementations & benchmarks**
  Real‑time approximations are implemented in game engines as “GPU fluids” using 2D or 3D textures for velocity and density, updated via compute shaders; browser‑based demos use WebGL fragment shaders for 2D fluids. Eulerian–Lagrangian methods like Particle Flow Maps show improved long‑range transport versus pure semi‑Lagrangian advection in benchmarks on incompressible flows, which matter for believable looping or extended aurora‑type animations. [dl.acm](https://dl.acm.org/doi/pdf/10.1145/3624011)

- **Strengths & limitations**
  Strengths include rich emergent detail, plausible large‑scale flows, and compatibility with texture‑based GPU pipelines. Limitations are complexity of implementation, difficulty in direct artistic control, sensitivity to numerical stability vs performance, and challenges mapping parameters (viscosity, vorticity confinement) to subjective qualities like “magical,” “calm,” or “aggressive.” [dl.acm](https://dl.acm.org/doi/pdf/10.1145/3624011)

### 4.2 Particle‑based motion systems

- **Theory & mechanism**
  Particle systems represent motion as collections of discrete entities with positions, velocities, and additional attributes (color, life, mass). Classical systems apply forces and integrate trajectories over time; more physically grounded variants use SPH or MPC to approximate fluid or complex fluid behaviors. SPH uses smoothing kernels to estimate local densities and gradients, which drive pressure and viscosity forces, while MPC alternates streaming and collision phases in a mesoscale statistical model. [arxiv](http://arxiv.org/pdf/0808.2157.pdf)

- **Literature evidence**
  Surveys on SPH and particle techniques in animation describe their use in shape modeling and fluid‑like phenomena, highlighting the trade‑off between visual richness and computational cost. DEM–LBM and related techniques demonstrate robust numerical modeling of particle–fluid coupling, supporting stylized depictions of particle‑laden flows and sedimentation that can inspire motion‑design presets. [pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC7123128/)

- **Implementations & benchmarks**
  Production engines expose particles via node‑based systems (e.g., Niagara, VFX Graph) with GPU instancing, where thousands to millions of particles update each frame. Benchmarks show that simplified force models and limited neighbor searches enable real‑time performance even for dense swarms, whereas full SPH requires more constrained resolutions or aggressive approximations. Browser‑based implementations often maintain particles as buffers consumed by vertex shaders, allowing interactive, stylized fields in WebGL. [ieeexplore.ieee](https://ieeexplore.ieee.org/document/8941044/)

- **Strengths & limitations**
  Strengths include granular control (emitters, forces, life‑cycle), natural support for discrete phenomena (sparks, debris, trails), and easy coupling with interactive events. Limitations are potential noise and instability, difficulty achieving smooth coherent flows without more advanced algorithms, and challenges in authoring and debugging when particle counts are large and behaviors interact nonlinearly. [icemm](https://www.icemm.com/en/particle-methods-in-cfd-simulation/)

### 4.3 Shader‑driven procedural fields

- **Theory & mechanism**
  Shader‑based motion treats the visual output as evaluation of analytic or pseudo‑random functions of space and time: e.g., noise‑based vector fields, signed distance functions for shapes, or sinusoidal displacement of vertices. Procedural displacement and surface shaders combine spatial coordinates and time as “texture coordinates” for 3D noise and other patterns, yielding complex, self‑similar, organic motion at interactive rates. [itn-web.it.liu](https://itn-web.it.liu.se/~stegu76/TNM084-2019/Insights_ch7.pdf)

- **Literature evidence**
  Chapters on procedural shading show that modern GPUs can render rich procedural patterns at fully interactive speeds, with GLSL well‑suited for such workloads. Web‑focused tutorials demonstrate how passing time uniforms into GLSL allows continuous animation of wave‑like or ripple‑like effects on meshes or full‑screen quads, illustrating the basic pattern for motion via shader‑level mathematics. Research prototypes like AI‑assisted shader evolution underscore the power and flexibility of fragment shaders to span broad aesthetic spaces in real time. [arxiv](https://arxiv.org/html/2512.08951)

- **Implementations & benchmarks**
  Common patterns include noise‑driven UV distortions, flow‑mapping (advecting textures by vector fields), and ray‑marched volumetric shapes in fragment shaders. Tutorials on animating WebGL shaders with GSAP show concrete patterns to bind JavaScript timelines to shader uniforms for coordinated reveals, ripples, and dynamic blur effects. Performance benchmarks indicate that analytic shaders often outperform simulation approaches when targeting purely aesthetic, non‑physically constrained motion. [tympanus](https://tympanus.net/codrops/2025/10/08/how-to-animate-webgl-shaders-with-gsap-ripples-reveals-and-dynamic-blur-effects/)

- **Strengths & limitations**
  Strengths are excellent performance on GPUs, full control over local motion laws, and compact code that can encode complex behavior. Limitations include steep learning curves, difficulty expressing global constraints (e.g., mass conservation) and inter‑object interactions, and sometimes brittle parameter spaces where small changes break aesthetics or performance. [arxiv](https://arxiv.org/html/2512.08951)

### 4.4 Procedural geometry and structure‑growth systems

- **Theory & mechanism**
  Procedural geometry systems—L‑systems, grammars, fractals—define structure as the outcome of recursive rules; time‑dependent evaluation yields growth or unfolding motion. Coupling such systems with fields or noise allows branching, clustering, and other organic behaviors that resemble plant growth, crack propagation, or urban expansion. [itn-web.it.liu](https://itn-web.it.liu.se/~stegu76/TNM084-2019/Insights_ch7.pdf)

- **Literature evidence**
  While much of the literature focuses on static modeling, real‑time rendering texts show that procedural displacement and surface shaders can be used to animate structural patterns by treating time as another input coordinate. This implies that classical geometric procedures can be repurposed as motion generators, especially when rendered via GPU shaders as evolving distance fields or instanced geometry. [itn-web.it.liu](https://itn-web.it.liu.se/~stegu76/TNM084-2019/Insights_ch7.pdf)

- **Implementations & benchmarks**
  In practice, real‑time implementations often keep rule evaluation on CPU (due to branching and recursion) and upload geometry or parameter buffers to the GPU for instanced rendering; stylized growth can also be approximated by incrementally revealing precomputed structures with shader‑driven masks. Performance scales with rule complexity and the number of generated instances, but many static‑structure approaches can be adapted to interactive growth by incremental evaluation and culling. [itn-web.it.liu](https://itn-web.it.liu.se/~stegu76/TNM084-2019/Insights_ch7.pdf)

- **Strengths & limitations**
  Strengths include structurally rich motion (branching, fractal detail) and compatibility with symbolic or semantic control (e.g., rules for “veins,” “roads,” “branches”). Limitations are algorithmic complexity, sometimes poor mapping between rules and percepts, and difficulty ensuring temporal coherence and physically plausible inertia without additional controllers.

### 4.5 Physics‑based easing and spring dynamics

- **Theory & mechanism**
  Physics‑based easing uses ordinary differential equations, typically mass–spring–damper systems, to move values toward targets with inertia, overshoot, and damping. The canonical equation for a 1D spring–mass system subject to a target position involves terms for stiffness and damping; discretized integration yields motion that automatically respects continuity and exhibits natural easing without explicit keyframes. These models generalize beyond single scalars to multi‑dimensional configurations (camera rigs, UI layouts), where each degree of freedom is governed by springs. [dl.acm](https://dl.acm.org/doi/fullHtml/10.1145/3582437.3582485)

- **Literature evidence**
  Work on believability and timing shows that decomposing actions into anticipation, main action, and follow‑through stages yields more convincing motion, and that these stages can be parameterized in ways compatible with spring‑based controllers. Studies on exaggerated realistic motion indicate that timing deformations relative to physically realistic trajectories (e.g., timing of squash‑and‑stretch around impact) significantly affect perceived plausibility, supporting the use of physical baselines with controlled deviations. [sciencedirect](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0010027724001665)

- **Implementations & benchmarks**
  In practice, physics‑based easing appears in UI frameworks, game engines, and animation libraries, where developers specify parameters like stiffness and damping per property. Benchmarks indicate such systems are lightweight enough for large numbers of concurrent animated properties, making them ideal for responsive UI micro‑interactions. Combinations with GPU instancing (e.g., applying springs to many shader uniforms) allow swarm‑like behaviors with a small CPU footprint. [dl.acm](https://dl.acm.org/doi/fullHtml/10.1145/3582437.3582485)

- **Strengths & limitations**
  Strengths are intuitively “natural” motion, automatic continuity, and reuse across domains (UI, camera, object transforms). Limitations include difficulty mapping parameters to perceptual descriptors, interactions between coupled springs, and the risk of homogeneous “springy” aesthetics when overused. [sciencedirect](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0010027724001665)

### 4.6 Perception‑driven motion controllers

- **Theory & mechanism**
  Perception‑driven controllers treat psychophysical findings as primary design constraints: instead of simulating physics, they enforce kinematic signatures known to elicit animacy, causality, or plausibility. Examples include controllers that ensure temporal contiguity between cause and effect within thresholds that preserve Michotte‑style launching impressions, or timing of squash‑and‑stretch phases around impact based on empirically derived windows. [pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC10273680/)

- **Literature evidence**
  Reviews on perceiving animacy from kinematics argue for a “life‑detector” mechanism sensitive to relational motion patterns (e.g., chasing, goal‑directed motion, coordinated turnings), which can be parameterized as constraints on speed changes, path curvature, and interaction geometry. Experiments on exaggerated motion show how varying shape deformation and damping relative to physical expectations changes perceived realism and “cartooniness.” Models of believability and anticipation provide higher‑level templates for decomposing motion and timing transitions between stages. [pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC10273680/)

- **Implementations & benchmarks**
  While few production engines expose explicit “animacy controllers,” research prototypes implement parametric models where timing and deformation parameters are tuned and evaluated through user studies for perceived realism or believability. These can be wrapped around underlying physics or shader systems: for example, controlling impact timing of deformations while the trajectory itself remains physically simulated. [sciencedirect](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0010027724001665)

- **Strengths & limitations**
  Strengths are direct linkage between parameters and user perception, enabling targeted control over “alive vs mechanical,” “realistic vs exaggerated.” Limitations include sparse coverage of phenomena, reliance on specific lab conditions, potential cultural and context dependence, and a lack of production tooling that exposes these parameters transparently to designers. [pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC10273680/)

## 6. Open Problems & Gaps

- Integrated perceptual design spaces
  Current tools rarely connect simulation or shader parameters to perceptual outcomes like animacy, believability, or comfort, despite psychophysical evidence on timing thresholds and kinematic signatures. Mapping parametric spaces (e.g., stiffness, damping, deformation timing) to perceptual descriptors remains under‑explored. [dl.acm](https://dl.acm.org/doi/fullHtml/10.1145/3582437.3582485)

- Authoring abstractions for coupled phenomena
  Designers lack high‑level abstractions for combining particles, fluids, shader fields, and perception‑based controllers into coherent systems; most workflows remain node‑or script‑centric and low‑level. There is limited research on domain‑specific languages or visual grammars that treat motion as first‑class, composable structure. [ieeexplore.ieee](https://ieeexplore.ieee.org/document/8941044/)

- Real‑time, robust hybrid methods
  While hybrid Eulerian–Lagrangian and particle–fluid coupling methods exist, adapting them to predictable, cross‑platform real‑time contexts with guaranteed performance budgets remains challenging. Designers often revert to either purely aesthetic shaders or heavily simplified physics. [mdpi](https://www.mdpi.com/2305-7084/4/4/55/pdf)

- Evaluation and benchmarks for motion qualities
  Benchmarks typically measure numerical accuracy or frame time rather than experiential qualities such as perceived smoothness, control responsiveness, or “alive” feeling, leaving a gap between research and practice. Systematic user‑study frameworks tailored to interactive motion design are limited. [dl.acm](https://dl.acm.org/doi/pdf/10.1145/3624011)

- Tooling that exposes perceptual constraints
  Few authoring tools provide visual feedback on timing constraints (e.g., causal delays beyond which launching impressions break) or animacy cues; integrating simple perceptual guidelines could significantly improve outcomes. [pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC10273680/)

## 7. Conclusion

Computational motion design for interactive media spans a spectrum from physically grounded continuum and particle methods through analytic shader fields and procedural geometries to physically inspired and perception‑driven controllers. Each class offers distinct trade‑offs in visual richness, control, performance, and perceptual impact, and in practice effective systems often combine several, for example using shader‑driven fields to visualize velocities from simplified fluid solvers, or layering perception‑tuned deformations on top of physics‑based trajectories. [en.wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Computational_fluid_dynamics)

The current landscape shows strong foundations in numerical methods and shader programming, alongside growing insight into motion perception, but comparatively weak integration between these domains in tools and workflows. Further work in perceptually grounded parameterizations, authoring abstractions, and experiential benchmarks could help motion designers navigate the space more systematically and exploit the full expressive potential of computational motion design. [sciencedirect](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0010027724001665)
